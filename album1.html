"Born to Die" and "Blue Jeans"

Lana Del Rey’s “Born to Die” and “Blue Jeans,” both from her first major-label studio album Born to Die: The Paradise Edition (2012), establish her melancholic “Sad Girl” aesthetic by blending sultry vocals and cinematic instrumentals to delve into the themes of existential longing and loss of a lover. Lana’s inner conflict pertaining to these topics exemplifies the fact that "...so often in Lana’s songs (and in real life), the feeling of love sustains despite outrageous behavior," (Grande 209).

In “Born to Die,” Lana’s pessimistic love story details Lana’s desire for the warmth of another, no matter the cost, because we are all “Born to Die” anyways. Accompanying the steady beats and orchestral strings, Lana sings:

Keep makin' me laugh, let's go get high

The road is long, we carry on

Try to have fun in the meantime

"Born to Die" and "Blue Jeans"

For “Blue Jeans,” Lana also displays the need for a relationship, no matter if it is toxic or not. Her need for a man that fits her better “… than [her] favorite sweater,” outweighs the potential emotional damage from her lover’s feigned loyalty. With the sparse percussion and guitar riffs, Lana notes:

And I know that love is mean (Oh-oh) and love hurts (Oh-oh)

But I still remember that day we met in December, oh baby

"Born to Die" and "Blue Jeans"

Through the exploration of these themes, Lana creates a world where beauty and pain can coexist, which can be comparable to reality. Therefore, rather than glorifying the toxicity of these relationships, Lana is portraying a uniquely realistic perspective on the cultural issue of domestic abuse, as she is directly applying “…her past story for her writing inspiration,” in this specific album, as Yulia Rakhmawati notes in her analysis, “Lana Del Rey’s View of American Glimmering Lifestyle and American Dream Reflected In Born to Die: The Paradise Edition Album: A Genetic Structuralism Study,” (Rakhmawati 293).
