"Ultraviolence" and "Pretty When You Cry"

In “Ultraviolence” and “Pretty When You Cry,” songs from Lana’s second major-label studio album Ultraviolence (2014), Lana maintains her unhopeful and joyless persona whilst singing of a tragic romance with the “bad boy” archetype, without much reflection on her own well-being. This furthermore illustrates the notion that Lana’s morals, “…despite its Gnostic and idealistic tendencies,” seems to be rooted on “…both sexual satisfaction and unity with the beloved,” (Grande 199-200). Furthermore, the progression between Born to Die (2012) and Ultraviolence (2014) exemplifies Lana’s critical realization that she must move on from her physically and mentally abusive relationship(s).

In “Ultraviolence,” Lana captures an outwardly controversial scene; she vividly depicts a physically violent relationship, with her loyalty to her lover, “Jim” or “Jim Beam,” washing away any negative connotations towards the situation. With a dream-like soundscape, Lana sings:

Jim told me that, he hit me and it felt like a kiss

Jim brought me back, reminded me of when we were kids (0:36-0:52)

For “Pretty When You Cry,” Lana illustrates the typical unreciprocated relationship; however, Lana learns that the love that she gives to these men is never enough, and that women should not degrade themselves into subservience, even if it is the gendered expectation. With a distant guitar and a raw, unembellished voice, Lana notes:

All those special times I spent with you, my love

They don’t mean shit compared to all your drugs (1:28-1:44)

With a deeper exploration of the same themes, Lana furthermore establishes her role as a poet that not only provides a realistic perspective to a rising cultural issue but actively defines feminism. In fact, her definition of a feminist is “...a woman who feels free enough to do whatever she wants,” according to Galant Nanta Adhitya and Nadia Lasari’s deep dive on Ultraviolence (2014), titled “Desiring Domination: A Postfeminist Study on the Lyrics of Lana Del Rey’s Ultraviolence Album,” (Adhitya & Lasari 44). Thus, when choosing to finally quit her bittersweet love despite being previously adamant in her position, she is actively against the violence portrayed in the songs.
